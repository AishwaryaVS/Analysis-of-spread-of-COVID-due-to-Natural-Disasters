Understanding the Impact of Natural Disasters on COVID-19

The objective of this project is to utilize epidemiological, clinical, and geographic data from two natural disaster case studies from the COVID-19 pandemic in the United States (2020-2022) to determine whether natural disasters affected the pandemic’s spread in affected regions. We also intend to determine whether the evacuation of residents from affected regions impacted COVID-19’s spread in neighboring regions.

Conclusion: From the results of our statistical testing, we see that our hypthesis holds true. Natural disasters do have an impact on the spread of the Covid-19 pandemic. From the initial statistical analysis using two sampled T test we found that there was some irregularity in the rise in number of cases in the affected states. The onset of the natural disaster did have an affect in the rise of COVID cases in the selected states. Due to the higher correlation of population of states rather than the distance of states to disaster locations to their pandemic unpredictability following natural disasters, the ARIMA test determines that the hypothesis that a high RMSE is a certain indicator of a natural disaster occurring in the state in question or in a nearby state where evacuation may result in expedited spreading of such a pandemic. In addition, further attempts at the ARIMA test would need to account for population-density and per- capita infections in order to control population-related variables and minimize the influence of other non-disaster factors in a pandemic’s changes
